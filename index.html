Market Summary — May 2026

The European dairy market is navigating a period of supply-side expansion and price bifurcation. Strong raw milk deliveries across key EU member states — Germany up nearly 7% year-on-year in Q1 2026 — have pushed SMP and butter output to record or near-record levels. This abundance is a primary driver of the sustained butter price correction from the exceptional highs of 2025.

Meanwhile, the whey and whey-derived ingredient segment is telling an altogether different story. Sweet whey powder (food grade) has more than doubled in value versus May 2025, driven by robust demand for WPC 80 and whey protein concentrates globally. Whey concentrate remains in very tight supply, with factories fully committed on short-term positions. Forward demand for Q3 and Q4 2026 is active and well-supported.

On the demand side, the current heatwave is providing a short-term stimulus to fresh dairy consumption. Seasonal cheese demand for Southern European tourism season is adding incremental volume support. China's overall dairy import volumes remain mixed — with significant declines in SMP and WMP imports in January 2026, but notable increases in casein(ate)s and condensed milk — reflecting strategic procurement behaviour rather than structural demand weakness.

The EUR/USD rate near 1.17 — substantially stronger than the early 2025 low of ~1.02–1.04 — is compressing the EUR-equivalent value of USD-denominated global benchmark prices and making EU origins comparatively less competitive in third-country markets. This remains a structural headwind for EU exporters in H2 2026.

Supply & Milk Pool

EU-27 & Key Countries

Germany: Milk deliveries remained on an expansive trajectory through the first half of May 2026, reaching a year-on-year surplus of +5.9% in calendar week 20. The seasonal peak is now considered broadly reached. An anticipated EU-wide heatwave is expected to dampen milk output in the coming weeks, as heat stress reduces cow yield and milk fat/protein content — consistent with historical seasonal patterns. Q1 2026 total cow milk intake at dairy facilities stood at 8.45 million tonnes, up 6.9% year-on-year.

EU-27: The overall EU milk pool has been expanding at +4.4% year-on-year as of March 2026, representing a slight deceleration from the +4.8% pace recorded in February. Production of butter (+10.4%) and SMP (+15.5%) in Jan-Feb 2026 versus the same period last year reflects deliberate processor allocation toward the most marginal products. France showed a slight negative divergence in April, with deliveries just below year-ago levels.

Global context: The US continued to expand output in April 2026 (+2.7% YoY), supported by record dairy cow numbers at 9.65 million head. Australia (+4.1% YoY in April) and New Zealand (+6.0% YoY in March) are also contributing to a well-supplied global supply picture. The ife Raw Material Value for German milk fell to 37.5 Ct/kg in May 2026 — down 23.5% versus May 2025 — reflecting the divergence between rising SMP and falling butter values.

Whey & SWP Focus

BMi Core Products

Market Headline: Sweet whey powder (food grade) continues its powerful multi-month uptrend, with the German Kempten monthly average reaching 1.810 €/kg in May 2026 — an increase of +18.1% versus April and +58.9% versus May 2025. The market for near-term delivery is essentially cleared, with processors fully committed and buyers having to look to Q3/Q4 for new supply. This scarcity of near-term whey powder is not expected to ease rapidly, given strong WPC processing demand upstream.

Whey Concentrate: Whey permeate concentrate (the key feedstock for WPC 80 and WPI production) remains persistently sought after. ZMB/AMI confirms that demand in the WPC segment is driving continued price appreciation in whey concentrate. Processors with access to whey streams are directing material toward the higher-value protein fraction, tightening available loose SWP supply.

Feed Grade SWP: Stabilising at the season end, with demand for feed-grade material easing as the calf-rearing season concludes. Prices remain broadly stable week-on-week at 1.430–1.510 €/kg.

Structural driver — WPC demand: The global protein supplement and clinical nutrition markets continue to absorb WPC 80 and WPI at elevated rates. This sustained end-use demand is pulling more whey streams into protein processing, competing directly for the same whey inputs that produce SWP. The net effect is a persistent structural tightening of SWP availability, which — combined with strong near-term buyer urgency — has sustained the current price uptrend.

Forward outlook: With factories fully committed on short positions and buyers seeking Q3–Q4 2026 volumes, the technical supply-demand balance for food-grade SWP remains constructive. The approaching summer slowdown in milk collections (due to seasonal heat) will further constrain new whey availability. Buyers requiring SWP in H2 2026 are advised to engage in procurement discussions promptly.

Global Trade

EU Exports · China · Germany Q1 2026

Germany Q1 2026: German dairy exports showed broad-based growth in the first quarter of 2026. Butter exports surged +48.1% year-on-year to 74,717 tonnes. SMP exports grew +22.5% to 94,720 tonnes, with third-country exports up 53.7% — reaching 43.8% of total SMP shipments. Notably, SMP exports to Algeria grew strongly, while exports to Egypt more than doubled. Whey powder exports rose +9.7% to 77,237 tonnes, with 47.7% of volume going to non-EU-27 destinations — a significant increase of +36.8%. Lactose exports remained essentially flat (+0.6%).

EU-27 Trade (Q1 2026): EU cheese exports grew moderately (+0.9% YoY) to 336,245 tonnes. SMP exports expanded by 5.6% to 197,134 tonnes. Butter and butteröil exports recovered strongly (+29.0%) to 74,825 tonnes. However, whey powder exports from the EU declined 5.7% YoY, while WMP exports fell 19.2%. The EU's import side saw cheese (+6.5%) and butter (+22.4%) gains, reflecting continued intra-regional trade dynamics.

China Import Trends (January 2026 vs January 2025): China's overall dairy import picture is mixed. Cheese imports rose strongly (+39%), reflecting growing consumer interest in cheese categories. SMP and WMP imports fell sharply (both −36%) — suggesting destocking of inventory built in prior periods or price resistance. Casein(ate)s imports surged +131%, possibly linked to technical ingredient procurement. Lactose imports declined 20%, with EU27 accounting for 27–33% of Chinese lactose supply.

Feed & Energy Markets

Dairy Cost Drivers

Feed markets — favourable for European dairy farmers: European feed grain prices have softened meaningfully compared to the elevated levels of recent years. MATIF milling wheat futures (September 2026 delivery) were quoted at approximately €212 per tonne on 27 May 2026 — down from €213 on 25 May. MATIF corn futures (June 2026) were priced at approximately €219 per tonne. These price levels represent a relatively benign feed cost environment for EU dairy farmers, supporting milk margin resilience even as farmgate milk prices are easing from their 2025 highs.

Energy — elevated but easing: Brent crude oil fell sharply on 27 May, dropping more than 4% to near $94–96 per barrel, as optimism grew around potential US-Iran peace negotiations and the possible reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. This represents a near-five-week low and — if geopolitical resolution materialises — could provide meaningful relief to energy-intensive dairy processing operations (drying, refrigeration, transport). At current EUR/USD rates (~1.163), Brent crude translates to approximately €81–83 per barrel in EUR terms.

Milk/feed ratio: With the ife Raw Material Value of German milk falling to 37.5 Ct/kg in May 2026 (down from 49.0 Ct/kg in May 2025), and feed costs remaining moderate, the milk-to-feed price ratio is currently under mild pressure — primarily driven by the sharp butter price correction rather than feed cost increases. Dairy farmer profitability will depend on forward farmgate price developments and the pace of the seasonal milk production decline.

FX Markets

EUR Impact on Dairy Trade

EUR/USD — Strong euro weighing on export competitiveness: The euro has strengthened substantially from the lows of early 2025 (near 1.02–1.04) to its current level around 1.163. The ECB reference rate stood at 1.1634 on 26 May 2026, with the May monthly average at approximately 1.1706. This appreciation — driven in part by relative economic recovery momentum in the Eurozone and moderated US rate expectations — makes EU-origin dairy products more expensive when priced in USD on world markets, creating a structural competitiveness headwind for exporters.

Practical implication: For a customer purchasing EU SMP at the current world quotation of USD 3,240/tonne, the EUR-equivalent price translates to approximately €2,785/tonne at today's EUR/USD 1.1634. One year ago, with EUR/USD near 1.12, the same USD price would have been equivalent to approximately €2,893/tonne — meaning the stronger EUR has compressed the EUR return on USD-priced exports by an estimated 3–4%.

EUR/GBP: The pound has been broadly stable versus the euro. EUR/GBP stood at 0.8638 on 26 May 2026 (April monthly average: 0.86933), meaning the euro costs slightly fewer pence compared to a year ago (April 2025: 0.85379). This is a moderate negative for UK importers of EU dairy, whose costs rise slightly in GBP terms.

EUR/PLN and EUR/CZK: Central European currencies remain relatively stable against the euro. EUR/PLN at 4.2315 (May average: 4.2502), EUR/CZK at 24.257 (May average: 24.381) — minimal volatility in these trade-relevant pairs.
